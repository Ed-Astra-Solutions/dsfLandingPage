High-Protein Vegetarian Foods in India

By Dion Samuel · Lifestyle & Transformation Coach · Updated 22 July 2026 · 6 min read

"I'm vegetarian, so I can't get enough protein" is the most common myth I hear in India — and it's wrong. With the right desi foods you can comfortably hit your protein target for muscle building or fat loss. Here's the full list, with numbers, and a sample day that crosses 100 g.

Protein is the nutrient that builds muscle, keeps you full while dieting, and protects your body when you're losing fat. Most people should aim for 1.6–2 g of protein per kg of body weight per day. For a 65 kg vegetarian that's about 105–130 g. Totally doable on Indian food — you just have to be a bit deliberate, because veg meals often skew carb-heavy by default.

The MVP is soya chunks. Rupee for rupee, nothing beats soya for protein. A 50 g dry serving gives ~26 g of protein for a few rupees. Add it to pulao, curry or a quick soya bhurji.

A sample 100g+ protein vegetarian day

  • Breakfast: 3 besan chilla + 1 bowl curd → ~20 g
  • Lunch: 2 roti + 1 katori dal + 100 g paneer sabzi + salad → ~30 g
  • Snack: 1 glass milk + handful of roasted chana → ~15 g
  • Dinner: 1 bowl rice + rajma + 50 g soya chunk curry + curd → ~35 g

That's roughly 100 g of protein from fully vegetarian, everyday Indian food — no exotic ingredients, no supplements required.

Three habits that make it easy

  • Anchor every meal with a protein. Ask "where's the protein?" before you sit down. Curd, dal, paneer, soya — pick one, every meal.
  • Add curd or milk as a default. It's the simplest way to add 8–10 g without cooking anything.
  • Keep soya and sprouts stocked. They're cheap protein insurance for days you're short.

Frequently asked questions

Which vegetarian food has the most protein in India?

Soya chunks, at about 52 g per 100 g dry. Paneer, tofu, dal, rajma, chana, curd and milk are other excellent everyday vegetarian sources.

Can a vegetarian build muscle?

Yes. Combining paneer, soya, dal, curd, milk, tofu and optionally whey easily reaches 1.6–2 g of protein per kg of body weight — enough to build muscle when paired with strength training.

Do I need whey protein powder?

No. It's a convenient top-up on busy days, but you can hit your protein target from whole foods alone. Treat it as optional, not essential.
